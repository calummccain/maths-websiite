Hyperbolic Geodesics

A typical exercise when working in with Riemann spaces is to find the geodesics. Geodesics are the shortest paths between and are the analogues of straight lines that we're used to in everyday life. I'm going to skip a couple of the meaty steps in deriving the tangent space definition for hyperbolic geodesics (possibly do later?) but I'll pick things up from there.

\[\mathbf{r} = \cosh(\theta) \mathbf{u} + \sinh(\theta) \mathbf{v} \]

where \(\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u} \rangle = 1\), \(\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0\), \(\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = -1\) and \(\theta \in (-\infty, \infty) \). This describes a geodesic through \(\mathbf{u}\) in the direction \(\mathbf{v}\) which is in the tangent space of the hyperboloid at \(\mathbf{u}\).

This is a nice result except that usually we have two points which we want our geodesic to pass through rather than a point an a direction. So let's say that our geodesic passes through \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) which are both on our hyperboloid; we now need to find the corresponding \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\). Firstly we can set \(\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{a}\) without too much trouble leaving us to find \(\mathbf{v}\) in terms of \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\). As the geodesic passes through \(\mathbf{b}\) for some value of \(\theta\) we get:

\[\mathbf{b} = \cosh(\theta) \mathbf{a} + \sinh(\theta) \mathbf{v} \Rightarrow \mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{b} - \cosh(\theta) \mathbf{a}}{\sinh(\theta)}\]

Neat! We've found both \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) so we're done right? Let's just check that that the inner products are satisfied:

\[\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle = 1\]

\[ \begin{aligned} \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle &= \left \langle \mathbf{a}, \frac{\mathbf{b} - \cosh(\theta) \mathbf{a}}{\sinh(\theta)} \right \rangle\\ &=\frac{1}{\sinh(\theta)}\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle - \frac{\cosh(\theta)}{\sinh(\theta)}\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle\\ &=\frac{1}{\sinh(\theta)}\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle - \frac{\cosh(\theta)}{\sinh(\theta)}\\ \end{aligned} \]

Hang on, this doesn't equal 0... but have a trick up our sleeves though as we have a bit of flexibilty with \(\theta\). By setting \(\cosh(\theta) = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle = \cosh(\alpha)\) i.e. \(\theta = \alpha\) where \(\alpha\) is the hyperbolic distance between \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\), we can get \(\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0\)!

Finally we just need to check \(\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = -1 \):

\[ \begin{aligned} \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle &= \left \langle \frac{\mathbf{b} - \cosh(\alpha) \mathbf{a}}{\sinh(\alpha)}, \frac{\mathbf{b} - \cosh(\alpha) \mathbf{a}}{\sinh(\alpha)} \right \rangle\\ &= \frac{\langle \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{b} \rangle - 2 \cosh(\alpha)\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle + \cosh(\alpha)^2 \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle}{\sinh(\alpha)^2}\\ &= \frac{1 - \cosh(\alpha)^2 + \sinh(\alpha)^2}{\sinh(\alpha)^2}\\ &= 0 \end{aligned} \]

Noice!

Putting these together we get a general geodesic from \(\mathbf{a}\) to \(\mathbf{b}\) is:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{r} &= \cosh(\theta) \mathbf{a} + \sinh(\theta) \frac{\mathbf{b} - \cosh(\alpha) \mathbf{a}}{\sinh(\alpha)}\\ &= \frac{\cosh(\theta) \sinh(\alpha) \mathbf{a} + \sinh(\theta) \mathbf{b} - \sinh(\theta) \cosh(\alpha) \mathbf{a}}{\sinh(\alpha)}\\ &= \frac{\sinh(\alpha - \theta) \mathbf{a} + \sinh(\theta) \mathbf{b}}{\sinh{\alpha}} \end{aligned} \]

A neat thing/check is to notice that if the points are close together (meaning \(\alpha\) is small) and \(\theta\) is small then:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{r} &= \frac{(\alpha - \theta) \mathbf{a} + \theta \mathbf{b}}{\alpha}\\ &= \left(1 - \frac{\theta}{\alpha} \right) \mathbf{a} + \frac{\theta}{\alpha} \mathbf{b} \\ &= (1 - \lambda) \mathbf{a} + \lambda \mathbf{b} \end{aligned} \]

Here we've set \(\frac{\theta}{\alpha} = \lambda\). This is the usual Euclidean equation for the straight line between two points which is what we expect!
