To generate a regular honeycomb ***LINK*** we need three integer parameters \(p\), \(q\), \(r\) all greater than 3. Writing these as \(\{p,q,r\}\) gives us the Coxeter symbol for the honeycomb. Each of the three parameters specify a particular aspect of the honeycomb:

  1. \(p\) - number of edges/vertices per face
  2. \(q\) - number of faces surrounding each vertex
  3. \(r\) - number of cells surrounding each edge

From these three numbers we can determine whether the resulting honeycomb exists in \(\mathbb{S}^3\), \(\mathbb{E}^3\) or \(\mathbb{H}^3\) by looking at the sign of \(\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{p}\right) \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{r}\right) - \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{q}\right)\) ****derived here****:

$$ \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{p}\right) \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{r}\right) - \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{q}\right) \begin{cases} \gt 0, & \mathbb{S}^3\\ = 0, & \mathbb{E}^3\\ \lt 0, & \mathbb{H}^3 \end{cases} $$

Solving this equation in each case gives the following:

Geometry Honeycombs
\(\mathbb{S}^3\) \(\{3,3,3\}, \{3,3,4\}, \{3,3,5\}, \{3,4,3\}, \{4,3,3\}, \{5,3,3\}\)
\(\mathbb{E}^3\) \(\{4,3,4\}\)
\(\mathbb{H}^3\) Otherwise
(proof of classification)

The \(\mathbb{S}^3\) honeycombs are usually called polychorons and are the 4D analogues of the platonic solids ****link***

The \(\mathbb{E}^3\) honeycomb \(\{4,3,4\}\) is more commonly recognised as Minecraft and is a 3-way infinite grid of cubes.

The \(\mathbb{H}^3\) honbeycombs consist of all the leftover combinations of \(\{p,q,r\}\). Despite the fact that they are much more common, they're arguably the most interesting and are of active study in Knot theory, Topology and Number theory.

Each of the these three groups have very different flavour ***link* mainly due to the fact that the underlying spaces have different curvatures:

Geometry \(M\) Curvature \(K\)
\(\mathbb{S}^3\) \(+1\)
\(\mathbb{E}^3\) \(0\)
\(\mathbb{H}^3\) \(-1\)

In order to "see" the \(\mathbb{S}^3\) and \(\mathbb{H}^3\) honeycombs we need to project them to \(\mathbb{R}^3\) (i.e. to 3D cartesian coordinates). This is because \(\mathbb{S}^3\) and \(\mathbb{H}^3\) are 3-manifolds non isometric to \(\mathbb{R}^3\) and so don't naturally embed with in the Euclidean metric. We don't need to do anything with \(\mathbb{E}^3\) as is is isometric to \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

For \(\mathbb{S}^3\) we are going to use the stereographic projection which maps:

$$ (w, x, y, z) \rightarrow \left(\frac{x}{1+w}, \frac{y}{1+w}, \frac{z}{1+w}\right) $$

This is a fairly standard mapping and more can be found here: ****LINK****

This isn't the only way to group the honeycombs. It turns out that they naturally fall into classes formed by fixing \(p\), \(q\) in \(\{p,q,r\}\) and varying \(r\). For example this gives the cubic honeycomb family:

$$ \{4,3,3\}, \{4,3,4\}, \{4,3,5\}, \{4,3,6\}, \{4,3,7\}, \dots $$

Viewing these together shows how the characteristics of the cell change when going from \(\mathbb{S}^3\) to \(\mathbb{E}^3\) to \(\mathbb{H}^3\).

Spherical ones look rounder, euclidean ones look flat (although they only exist for \(\{4,3,4\}\)...) while hyperbolic ones look pointy and some even have their corners cut-off - more details here ****LINK****.
